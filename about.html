About

Educator. Learning scientist. Still a student of both.

I started in government schools in India — classrooms with far more students than resources, where the question of who gets to learn well, and who doesn't, was never abstract.

That question followed me through an M.S.Ed. at the University of Pennsylvania and into twelve years of instructional design in EdTech and higher education. I currently work as a Senior Instructional Designer at Western Governors University, where I help subject-matter experts turn what they know into learning experiences that actually transfer to the workplace — not just to a test.

What I actually believe about learning

Learning isn't information delivered — it's a change produced through practice, feedback, and reflection. Retrieval beats re-reading. Spacing beats cramming. Interleaving builds understanding that a single well-organized unit never will. These aren't slogans for me; they're the checklist I run every piece of content through before I call it done.

Why "positionality" shows up in my work a lot

Two students can receive the exact same scholarship information and walk away with completely different reactions — one thinks "I should apply," the other thinks "I don't belong here." Same information, different reality. Language, geography, family, and economics all shape what "opportunity" even means to the person standing in front of it. Good design starts by listening for that difference, not by assuming it away.

The through-line

Across a documentary, a STEM-inclusion course, an AI-literacy deck, and a teacher community of 10,000 people, the same question keeps showing up: who gets access, who gets to participate, and who actually gets agency over their own learning? This site is where I keep working on the answer.
